Moral Foundations Theory · MFT Profile
Haidt & Graham (2004–2012) · Six moral foundations scored per text · Cross-cultural affective architecture

Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt & Graham) holds that human moral intuitions are organised around six innate, universal but culturally weighted foundations: Care/Harm (empathy, protection of the vulnerable), Fairness/Cheating (justice, reciprocity, equality), Loyalty/Betrayal (group cohesion, in-group solidarity), Authority/Subversion (hierarchy, tradition, deference), Sanctity/Degradation (purity, sacredness, disgust), and Liberty/Oppression (autonomy, freedom from coercion). Each foundation is scored as a normalised ratio of positive to negative moral vocabulary detected in the text. High scores indicate the text foregrounds that moral dimension; low scores indicate indifference or active suppression. Cross-cultural differences are particularly visible here: Arabic institutional discourse typically weights Authority and Sanctity more heavily; Anglo-American discourse foregrounds Care and Fairness; Mediterranean cultures often balance Loyalty with Liberty.

MFT Radar · All 6 Foundations
Overlay of Texts A / B / C — normalised 0–100

Each axis represents one moral foundation. Values are normalised: 100 = maximum positive moral activation detected; 0 = no vocabulary present for that foundation. Triangles of similar shape indicate morally aligned texts; divergent shapes reveal distinct cultural-moral orientations. Note that a low score does not imply immoral content — it means the text simply does not invoke that moral channel.

Large within-group differences (e.g. Text A scoring high on Care while Text C scores high on Authority) signal genuine cross-cultural moral divergence rather than mere stylistic variation.

Comparative Overlay · Human Needs vs. Moral Foundations
Side-by-side radar comparison — same texts, two analytical lenses

These two radars place the Human Needs Profile (Magni, 2025 — existential-motivational layer) and the Moral Foundations Profile (Haidt & Graham — sociocultural-normative layer) side by side for each text. Where a text scores high on Sociality in the needs radar and high on Care and Loyalty in the MFT radar, it signals a coherent communitarian orientation. Where the two radars diverge — e.g. high Self-Actualisation (needs) but high Authority (MFT) — the text carries an internal tension: it promotes individual achievement within a strongly hierarchical normative frame. Such tensions are invisible to either framework alone.

Perceptual Module · NSM Primitives
Wierzbicka's semantic primitives detected in each text's native language · Affective valence per segment

Natural Semantic Metalanguage (NSM) theory holds that a small set of universal lexical primitives — BODY, PLACE, PEOPLE, PART, THING, and the mental predicates THINK, FEEL, WANT — underlies all human meaning. This table shows which primitive anchors are active in each text, along with the dominant affective valence (positive / negative / neutral) of the surrounding context. A primitive scored positive signals that the surrounding discourse frames it constructively; negative signals frustration or threat framing.

Semantic Module · 81-Configuration Matrix
Activation state of the four Human Needs for each text (Magni, 2025)

Each of the four axes (Survival, Security, Sociality, Self-Actualisation) receives a score of +1 (satisfied — the text actively promotes that need), 0 (neutral — the need is neither foregrounded nor suppressed), or −1 (frustrated — the text signals threat or deficiency). The combination of four ternary scores produces one of 81 possible neurosemantic configurations, each mapped to a named archetype. The archetype captures the dominant existential orientation of the text.

Inverted-U Trajectory · ICCI Readability Penalty
Optimal readability preserves semantic distinctiveness — both extremes are penalised

The ICCI integrates semantic complementarity with surface readability via an Inverted-U curve. Texts in the optimal Flesch-Kincaid range (FK 38–72) preserve both accessibility and symbolic richness. Texts above FK 72 are classified as over-simplified: excessive clarity erodes the distinctive, figurative depth of the message. Texts below FK 38 are below threshold: high complexity limits accessibility. The penalty is asymmetric — over-simplification is penalised 30% more severely than under-complexity, reflecting the framework's emphasis on preserving distinctive meaning.
